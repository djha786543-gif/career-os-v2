#6
Third-Party AI Risk Management High Wave 2
Every enterprise using Salesforce Einstein, Microsoft Copilot, ServiceNow AI, or Workday AI has inherited AI risk it hasn't formally assessed. EU AI Act creates explicit third-party AI supply chain obligations for high-risk systems. Traditional TPRM questionnaires weren't built for probabilistic, drifting, hallucinating AI systems.
Analyst Commentary
TPRM has existed as a discipline for over a decade, but almost none of its methodology was designed for AI systems. The core challenge: traditional vendor assessment asks "does this system work as specified?" — AI systems don't have a fixed specification, they have a probabilistic behavior distribution that changes as the model retrains. Questions about hallucination rates, training data provenance, model drift thresholds, and adversarial robustness don't appear in any existing vendor assessment questionnaire in widespread use. EU AI Act Article 28 creates explicit obligations on deployers of third-party AI systems — requiring organizations to formally assess and document AI risks in their supply chain, regardless of whether they built the AI themselves. This is the compliance mandate that will force TPRM methodology to evolve.
DJ's Strategic Position
Your SOX ITGC vendor management background is the foundation — you understand the assessment lifecycle, contract controls, and audit evidence requirements. The required augmentation is AI-specific assessment methodology on top of an existing skill, not a discipline from scratch.

#10
Continuous Compliance Architecture Emerging Wave 2
The era of annual compliance is ending. Real-time attestations, machine-readable policies, and automated compliance monitoring are now expected by regulators. Designing continuous AI control monitoring is a fundamental methodology shift — the entire SOX periodic testing model doesn't map to AI systems that drift and retrain continuously. Almost nobody is credentialed in this yet.
Analyst Commentary
Annual compliance was designed for systems with fixed configurations — audit once, certify for a year. AI systems retrain continuously, drift from their baseline behavior, and make decisions differently than they did six months ago. Applying a once-a-year audit cadence to these systems is like auditing a river by testing the water once annually. This creates a new discipline: continuous AI control monitoring architecture — designing systems that can attest to AI model behavior in near-real-time, detect drift from governance thresholds, and trigger automated remediation or human escalation. No major credentialing body has codified this yet. The ISACA and IIA are actively developing curriculum in this area (projected Q3 2027). Whoever publishes a practitioner methodology before that certification lands will be cited as the reference framework.
